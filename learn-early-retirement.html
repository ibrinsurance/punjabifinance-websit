Early Retirement in Canada

Retiring before 65 in Canada is achievable — but it requires understanding how CPP, OAS, RRSP, and TFSA interact when you stop working early.

What Early Retirement Actually Means in Canada

Early retirement in Canada means stopping paid employment before age 65 — the standard CPP/OAS eligibility age. You might retire at 45, 50, or 55. The challenge isn't just saving enough money — it's bridging the gap until government benefits kick in, and building income that lasts 40+ years.

The three phases of early retirement in Canada:
— Phase 1 (Early years, e.g., age 45–60): Living entirely off savings — TFSA withdrawals, RRSP meltdown strategy, non-registered investments, rental income
— Phase 2 (Age 60–70): Can start reduced CPP at 60 (36% less than at 65), or delay CPP to 70 for maximum benefit
— Phase 3 (Age 70+): CPP + OAS + RRIF withdrawals, reduced need to draw from portfolio

How much do you need? The 4% rule: your portfolio should be 25x your annual expenses. If you need $50,000/year, you need $1.25 million. In Canada, factor in that CPP and OAS will eventually reduce your drawdown — so you might need less than the pure 4% rule suggests.

CPP and OAS Timing Strategy for Early Retirees

CPP can start as early as age 60 — but with a 36% permanent reduction (0.6% per month before 65). Starting at 70 gives you 42% more than at 65. The breakeven point is roughly age 82–83.

Decision framework for early retirees:
— If you retire at 45 and have strong savings: delay CPP to 70, keep portfolio intact, let CPP grow
— If you retire at 55 with modest savings: start CPP at 65 to reduce portfolio drawdown
— Health matters: if family history suggests shorter life expectancy, take CPP earlier
— Consider the tax implications: taking CPP while also doing RRSP meltdowns stacks income

OAS starts at 65 minimum (can delay to 70 for 36% more). OAS is clawed back at $86,912+ income (2024). If your retirement income is below this threshold, you get full OAS. Plan RRSP meltdowns to stay under this number.

Example: Amrit retires at 50 with $1.2M. He delays CPP to 70, does a slow RRSP-to-TFSA meltdown in his 50s at low tax rates, and lets his TFSA grow tax-free. At 70, CPP + OAS covers $30,000/year, and he only needs $20,000 from portfolio — dramatically extending portfolio longevity.

The RRSP Meltdown Strategy for Early Retirees

If you retire early, you have a golden window: years between retirement and age 72 (when RRSP converts to RRIF mandatorily) to withdraw from your RRSP at low tax rates — especially if you have no employment income.

The strategy: Withdraw $40,000–$50,000/year from RRSP in your 50s and 60s, converting it to TFSA room (re-contribute to TFSA each year). Pay minimal tax now instead of massive tax when forced withdrawals start at 72 and OAS/CPP is also adding to income.

Why this matters: Harjot retires at 52 with $600,000 RRSP and $200,000 TFSA. If she does nothing, by 72 her RRSP is worth $1.5M (at 5% growth). Mandatory RRIF withdrawals of $80,000+/year push her into the 33% bracket + OAS clawback. Instead, she withdraws $45,000/year from RRSP at ~15% average tax, moves it to TFSA, and by 72 has $300K RRSP and $600K TFSA. Lower taxes, more TFSA, better outcome.

Key rule: Don't withdraw more than fills the $53,359 (2024) basic personal amount + Ontario basic amount. Effective tax on this amount is near zero. Withdraw up to about $50,000–$55,000/year with minimal tax if it's your only income.

Healthcare, Benefits, and What Early Retirees Miss

The biggest gap early retirees don't plan for: employer benefits. When you leave your job, you lose group health/dental insurance. Before 65, you're on provincial health (OHIP in Ontario covers basics) but pay out-of-pocket for dental, vision, prescriptions, physiotherapy, and extended care.

Solutions for early retirees:
— Individual health insurance: Manulife, Sun Life, and Blue Cross offer individual plans. Expect $250–$500/month for a couple with dental. Apply within 60–90 days of losing group coverage (no medical underwriting window)
— HSA (Health Spending Account): If you do any consulting or self-employment, set up a corporation with HSA to deduct medical expenses
— Provincial drug coverage: ODB in Ontario kicks in at 65; before that, you pay

Budget for healthcare costs: Many early retirees underestimate this. Plan for $5,000–$10,000/year per couple before 65 in dental, prescriptions, and supplemental care.

Other considerations: Life insurance is cheaper at 45 than 65 — lock in before retiring if you have dependants. Disability insurance ends when you retire (no income to replace). Critical illness insurance should be considered during the early retirement window. Mental health support and community: many early retirees underestimate the psychological shift from working identity to retired life.

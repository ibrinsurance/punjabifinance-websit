🤔What Is FIRE?

FIRE — Financial Independence, Retire Early — is a movement and lifestyle philosophy centered on saving and investing aggressively during working years to accumulate enough wealth to live indefinitely on investment returns, without relying on employment income. The "retire early" part doesn't necessarily mean doing nothing — many FIRE adherents stop working full-time in their 30s or 40s and pursue purpose-driven work, entrepreneurship, or volunteering without financial pressure.

The foundation of FIRE is the 4% rule (also called the "safe withdrawal rate"): research from the Trinity Study (and numerous subsequent analyses) suggests that withdrawing 4% of your portfolio value in year one of retirement, then adjusting for inflation each year, has historically sustained a portfolio for 30+ years in most market scenarios. The implication: to replace $50,000/year of living expenses, you need $1.25 million invested ($50,000 ÷ 0.04). To replace $80,000/year, you need $2 million. Your "FIRE number" is your annual expenses divided by 0.04.

📋Types of FIRE

LeanFIRE: Achieving financial independence on a minimal budget — often $25,000–$40,000/year for a couple. Requires a smaller portfolio ($625,000–$1M) but demands ongoing frugality and is vulnerable to any significant expense increase. Common in low cost-of-living areas or households without children.

FatFIRE: Financial independence with a generous lifestyle budget — often $100,000+/year. Requires a larger portfolio ($2.5M+) but provides substantial financial flexibility, room for healthcare costs in later years, and ability to adapt to life changes. More common among higher-earning professionals.

BaristaFIRE / Coast FIRE: Partially retire early by accumulating enough invested assets that compound growth alone will reach your full FIRE number by traditional retirement age — without any additional contributions. You work part-time or in lower-pressure work to cover current living expenses while your existing investments grow toward the target. This hybrid approach reduces both the time to "semi-retirement" and the psychological difficulty of extreme savings rates.

🔢The FIRE Math

Your savings rate is the dominant variable in how quickly you can reach FIRE — more important than investment returns. At a 10% savings rate, you need 40+ years to reach financial independence. At 25%, approximately 32 years. At 50%, approximately 17 years. At 75%, approximately 7 years. The relationship is non-linear: the highest savings rates compress the timeline dramatically because you're simultaneously reducing the portfolio size needed (lower expenses = lower FIRE number) and accelerating accumulation.

FIRE number calculation: (Annual living expenses) ÷ 0.04 = Portfolio needed. Example: a family spending $60,000/year needs $1.5M. If they currently have $200,000 and add $30,000/year at 7% average return, they reach $1.5M in approximately 18 years. Reducing spending to $45,000/year changes the target to $1.125M and might be reached in 14 years — the compounding of both a smaller target and slightly higher savings rate significantly shortens the journey.

🇨🇦FIRE in the Canadian Context

FIRE in Canada has specific considerations that differ from the US-centric origins of the movement. Healthcare: provincial health insurance removes the enormous variable of employer-sponsored healthcare that constrains US early retirees. Leaving employment in Canada doesn't leave you without healthcare — provincial coverage continues regardless of employment status, though supplemental dental/drug coverage requires a private plan or group benefits.

Registered accounts in a Canadian FIRE context: TFSA withdrawals don't count as income (no OAS clawback risk, no impact on income-tested benefits). RRSP/RRIF withdrawals count as taxable income. For early retirees, careful sequencing — drawing from taxable accounts first, converting some RRSP to RRIF gradually in low-income years — minimizes lifetime tax. A FIRE portfolio structured primarily around TFSAs provides the most tax-efficient drawdown.

⚖️Is FIRE Realistic?

FIRE is achievable for a minority of Canadians, particularly those with high incomes relative to cost of living, dual-income households, and strong commitment to below-average consumption. For most immigrant families in high-cost cities like Toronto or Vancouver, the combination of housing costs, childcare, and potential remittance obligations makes extreme savings rates difficult but not impossible.

The FIRE mindset — intentional spending, maximizing savings rate, building investment knowledge — has value even for people who don't aspire to full early retirement. Working toward "FI" (financial independence, without the "RE" of early retirement) means building enough invested assets that work becomes optional rather than mandatory. This reduces financial anxiety, enables career risk-taking, and provides resilience against job loss. The discipline required for FIRE applied more moderately produces meaningful financial outcomes for anyone who adopts it.

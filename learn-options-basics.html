Options Trading Basics for Canadians

Options are powerful financial instruments — and commonly misunderstood. Here's what they are, how they work, and whether you should trade them.

What Are Stock Options

A stock option is a contract giving you the right — but not the obligation — to buy or sell 100 shares of a stock at a specific price before a specific date. Unlike buying a stock, you're buying a right.

Two types:
— Call option: The right to buy 100 shares at the strike price before expiry. You buy calls when you think the stock will rise.
— Put option: The right to sell 100 shares at the strike price before expiry. You buy puts when you think the stock will fall (or to hedge existing holdings).

Key terms:
— Strike price: The price at which you can buy or sell the underlying stock
— Expiry date: The date the option expires (weekly, monthly, or up to 2 years out)
— Premium: The price you pay for the option contract (per share × 100 shares = total cost)
— In the money (ITM): Option has intrinsic value (call: stock above strike; put: stock below strike)
— Out of the money (OTM): Option has no intrinsic value yet

Simple example: Shopify is at $100. You buy a call option with a $110 strike expiring in 60 days for $3.00 premium (costs $300 total). If Shopify rises to $130 by expiry, your option is worth $20.00 ($130 − $110), and you profit $1,700 ($2,000 value − $300 cost). If Shopify stays at $100 or falls, your option expires worthless and you lose the $300 premium.

The Risk Profile of Buying Options

Buying options has a capped downside and theoretically unlimited upside — but the odds are stacked against the buyer. Here's why:

Time decay (theta): Every day that passes, your option loses value even if the stock doesn't move. This "time decay" accelerates as expiry approaches. An option with 60 days to expiry loses value faster per day than one with 180 days. Most options expire worthless — statistically, 70–80% of options bought expire with zero value.

Implied volatility: Option prices rise when the market expects volatility and fall when calm. Buying options during high volatility (earnings season, major news events) means you're paying a high premium — the stock needs to move significantly just for you to break even. This is a major disadvantage retail traders often underestimate.

The breakeven calculation: If you buy a call at a $3 premium with $110 strike, you need the stock above $113 (strike + premium) at expiry just to break even. Anything below $113 = a loss. Above $113 = profit proportional to how far above.

Leverage cuts both ways: Options allow controlling $10,000 worth of stock for $300. This leverage amplifies gains spectacularly on paper — and losses in reality. Most retail options traders lose money. Options are not a wealth-building tool for most people; they're a speculation tool for professionals.

Selling Options — The Other Side

Selling options is where sophisticated investors often profit. When you sell an option, you collect the premium upfront but take on the obligation to fulfill the contract if exercised. This sounds risky but can be managed.

Covered calls — the most beginner-friendly strategy: You own 100 shares of a stock. You sell a call option against those shares, collecting premium. If the stock rises above the strike, you sell your shares at the strike price (you "miss out" on extra upside but keep the premium). If the stock stays flat or falls, you keep the premium as extra income.

Example: You own 100 RBC shares at $135. You sell a call with $145 strike expiring in 30 days and collect $1.50 premium ($150 total). If RBC stays below $145, you keep your shares and the $150. If RBC rises to $150, you sell your shares at $145 (a good price you were happy with) plus keep the $150 premium. The risk: if RBC drops to $120, you still own the shares at a loss (partially offset by $150 premium).

Cash-secured puts: You sell a put option on a stock you'd be happy owning, keeping the cash to cover the purchase. If the stock falls to the strike, you buy it at the strike price (which you were okay with). If it stays above, you keep the premium. This is a way to potentially buy stocks you like at a discount — or just generate income while waiting.

Should You Trade Options? Honest Canadian Advice

For the vast majority of Canadian investors, options are unnecessary and likely harmful to returns. Here's an honest assessment:

Reasons to avoid options if:
— You haven't maxed your TFSA and RRSP first
— You don't have a stable core portfolio of index ETFs
— You are easily emotional about market movements
— You don't have time to actively monitor positions
— You're learning investing — options add significant complexity to an already complex skill

Options may be appropriate if:
— You have a stable core portfolio and treat options as a small speculation bucket (under 10% of portfolio)
— You understand all the Greeks (delta, theta, vega, gamma) and can model your position's behaviour
— You're using covered calls on existing holdings to generate income (conservative strategy)
— You're using protective puts to hedge a large concentrated position

Where to learn more before risking real money:
— Paper trade first: Most brokers offer paper trading (simulated trading with no real money)
— Questrade and Interactive Brokers both allow options trading with proper approvals
— "The Options Playbook" (free online) is the best free educational resource
— CIRO (Canadian regulator) requires options approval on your brokerage account — they ask about experience and financial situation for good reason

Bottom line: Most retail options traders would have been better off just buying index ETFs. Options aren't a shortcut to wealth — they're a sophisticated instrument that requires significant knowledge to use safely.

🤔What Is Dollar-Cost Averaging?

Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) is the practice of investing a fixed dollar amount at regular intervals — weekly, bi-weekly, or monthly — regardless of whether the market is up or down. Instead of investing a lump sum all at once, you spread purchases over time, buying more units when prices are low and fewer when prices are high.

For example: you invest $500/month into a broadly diversified fund. In January, the price is $50/unit — you buy 10 units. In February, the price drops to $40/unit — you buy 12.5 units. In March, the price rises to $55/unit — you buy 9.1 units. Your average cost per unit ($48.56) is lower than the average price during the period ($48.33), because you automatically bought more shares when prices were lower. This mechanical effect is the mathematical basis of DCA's advantage during volatile periods.

✅Why It Works

DCA removes the pressure of market timing — one of the biggest behavioral investing mistakes. Most investors who wait for the "right time" to invest spend months or years on the sidelines, missing market returns while waiting for a dip that may not come, or buying after a run-up because the market finally "feels safe." Research by Dalbar consistently shows that individual investors significantly underperform the very funds they invest in, primarily because of poorly-timed entry and exit decisions.

DCA is also psychologically sustainable. Investing a fixed $500/month feels manageable even when markets are volatile. The discipline required to maintain contributions during a market crash — when every headline is negative and your portfolio has dropped 20% — is much lower with automated DCA than with discretionary investing. Automation is the key: set up automatic transfers on payday to your investment account, and the contribution happens before you have a chance to second-guess it.

⚖️DCA vs. Lump Sum

Research (including a well-known Vanguard study) shows that investing a lump sum immediately beats DCA approximately two-thirds of the time over long periods, because markets tend to rise over time — money invested immediately has more time in the market. If you receive a $50,000 inheritance or bonus and markets rise 10% over the next year, DCA costs you opportunities: the half you haven't invested yet earned nothing.

However, lump sum investing requires psychological fortitude. If you invest $50,000 in January and markets drop 30% by April, you may panic-sell at a loss. For investors who would make poor decisions with a lump sum during volatility, DCA over 6–12 months is psychologically superior even if mathematically suboptimal. The best strategy is the one you can execute consistently without panic — and for most investors, automatic monthly contributions sustained through market cycles beats theoretically optimal lump sum investing that gets abandoned during the first bear market.

⚙️How to Set It Up

Set up automatic investment on payday through your brokerage account. Questrade, Wealthsimple Invest, and most Canadian brokerages allow automatic recurring purchases. Choose a frequency matching your pay schedule: bi-weekly payroll → bi-weekly investment transfers; monthly salary → monthly investment. Aligning investment timing with income reduces the chance of insufficient funds.

For RRSP and TFSA contributions, confirm you have contribution room before automating, and consider the annual contribution limits — automatic monthly deposits can accidentally over-contribute if you're not tracking room used. CRA My Account shows your current contribution room. Over-contributions trigger a 1% monthly penalty tax on excess amounts — costly and avoidable with simple tracking.

Insider Trading in Canada

Insider trading is illegal and vigorously prosecuted. Understanding what it is — and what's legal — protects you as an investor.

What Is Insider Trading

Insider trading is buying or selling a security based on material, non-public information (MNPI) about that company. "Material" means information that would affect a reasonable investor's decision to buy or sell. "Non-public" means information not yet released to the general public.

Classic examples:
— A CFO knows next week's earnings will be terrible and sells their shares before the report
— An investment banker working on a merger tells their brother to buy the acquisition target
— A doctor in a drug trial knows a medication failed and shorts the biotech company before announcement
— An employee overhears that their company is being acquired and buys shares

Who is an "insider" in Canada? The definition is broader than most people think. Securities law defines insiders as directors, senior officers, and shareholders owning 10%+ of a company. But "tipping" liability extends to anyone who receives and trades on MNPI — even if they have no official connection to the company. A random tip at a party can make you legally liable.

In Canada, securities regulation is provincial — the Ontario Securities Commission (OSC), BC Securities Commission, and AMF (Quebec) are the main regulators. They coordinate through the Canadian Securities Administrators (CSA).

How Regulators Detect and Prosecute Insider Trading

Market surveillance is automated and comprehensive. Every trade on the TSX is recorded, timestamped, and automatically flagged for unusual patterns — large options purchases, unusual volume, buying in accounts connected to company employees, or trades happening days before major announcements.

What triggers an investigation:
— Unusual trading volume in a stock before a major announcement
— Large options purchases (especially short-dated, out-of-money calls) before positive news
— Account connections: the OSC cross-references trading accounts against company employee lists, lawyers working on deals, and family connections
— Tipping patterns: if 10 accounts with no obvious connection all bought the same small stock before a takeover, algorithms flag the network

Famous Canadian cases:
— Poseidon Concepts fraud investigation (2012–2018): management inflated revenue before stock collapse
— OSC has levied hundreds of millions in penalties and bans in the last decade
— Canadian regulators share information with SEC, FINRA, and UK FCA for cross-border cases

Penalties in Canada: Up to $5 million fine per count and up to 5 years imprisonment under the Securities Act. The OSC can also permanently ban individuals from capital markets.

What Is Legal — Legal Forms of "Inside" Trading

Trading on public information is always legal. Reading an earnings report, analyzing public filings on SEDAR+, using fundamental analysis — all perfectly legal. The line is only crossed with non-public information.

Legal insider trading — executive disclosures: Company insiders (executives, directors) are legally allowed to buy and sell their own company's shares, but must report within 5 days of each trade. These are disclosed on SEDI (System for Electronic Disclosure by Insiders) at sedi.ca. Many investors track these disclosures as a signal — insiders buying their own stock is often bullish.

Automatic Trading Plans (10b5-1 equivalent in Canada): Executives can set up pre-scheduled trading plans before they possess MNPI. Once set up, trades execute automatically on a schedule regardless of what the executive knows later. This provides a legal safe harbour for executives who inevitably have MNPI at various times.

Research and "mosaic theory": Analysts who combine publicly available information — even non-material private conversations with management about non-material topics — to form a complete thesis are generally legal. The key: no single piece of the mosaic is material MNPI.

What You Should Actually Do as an Investor

If someone gives you a "hot tip," ask yourself:
— How does this person know this?
— Could this be material non-public information?
— Am I the first or second person to hear this?
If you can't answer these questions with confidence, don't trade on the tip. The legal risk is not worth any potential gain.

Common situations Canadian investors encounter:
— A friend who works at a public company mentions layoffs are coming: Don't trade until it's public
— An industry contact hints at a deal: Don't trade
— You hear at a family gathering that a merger is happening: Don't trade
— You read on Reddit or Twitter that a stock is going up: This is public information (even if rumors), generally legal to trade on, though risky to trust

Build a legal information edge: The legitimate way to have an edge is through research, discipline, and patience — not tips. Read every public filing on SEDAR+. Track insider trading disclosures on SEDI. Understand industries better than the average investor. Use free tools: TMX Money, Morningstar, Stockanalysis.com. This is harder than acting on a tip, but it's sustainable and legal.

Bottom line: The expected value of insider trading is always negative. The gains are limited (you can only make so much from one trade). The downside is career-ending fines, imprisonment, and permanent regulatory bans. And regulators have better tools for catching it than most people realize.

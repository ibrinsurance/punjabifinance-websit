The Canadian Housing Market — What You're Walking Into

Canada has some of the most expensive real estate in the world. In 2024, average home prices: — Toronto: ~$1.1 million — Vancouver: ~$1.3 million — Calgary: ~$590,000 — Ottawa: ~$620,000 — Edmonton: ~$400,000 — Halifax: ~$490,000

Despite high prices, homeownership remains the single biggest wealth-builder for most Canadian families. The average Canadian homeowner has significantly higher net worth than the average renter — not because owning is inherently smarter, but because it forces disciplined saving through mortgage payments and provides leveraged appreciation.

First-Time Buyer Programs — Don't Leave Free Money on the Table

FHSA — First Home Savings Account Introduced in 2023 — the best savings tool for first-time buyers: — Contribute up to $8,000/year, $40,000 lifetime — Tax deduction on contributions (like RRSP) — Withdrawals for home purchase are tax-free (like TFSA) — Opens at any Canadian financial institution

Example: Jaspreet contributes $8,000/year for 5 years = $40,000 in contributions + tax refunds of ~$3,200/year at a 40% marginal rate = $16,000 in tax savings. Her FHSA grows to ~$46,000 at 6% return. Tax-free withdrawal at purchase.

RRSP Home Buyer's Plan (HBP) — Withdraw up to $35,000 from RRSP tax-free for first home — Must repay over 15 years (or it becomes taxable income) — Combined with FHSA: access up to $75,000 in down payment savings

First Home Buyer's Tax Credit — Up to $10,000 × 15% = $1,500 federal tax credit — Apply on your tax return in the year of purchase

Land Transfer Tax (LTT) Rebate: — Ontario: Up to $4,000 rebate for first-time buyers — Toronto: Additional $4,475 Toronto LTT rebate — BC: Rebate on homes under $835,000

Down Payments and CMHC Mortgage Insurance

Minimum down payments in Canada: — Homes under $500,000: 5% minimum — Homes $500,000–$999,999: 5% on first $500K + 10% on remainder — Homes $1M+: 20% minimum (no insured mortgages)

CMHC Insurance (mortgage default insurance): Required when down payment is under 20%. Added to your mortgage: — 5–9.99% down: 4.0% premium — 10–14.99% down: 3.1% premium — 15–19.99% down: 2.8% premium

Example: $600,000 home, 5% down = $30,000 CMHC premium: ($570,000 × 4.0%) = $22,800 added to mortgage Total mortgage: $592,800

The CMHC trade-off: Paying CMHC insurance allows you to buy a home sooner with a smaller down payment — valuable in markets where prices may rise faster than you can save.

The Mortgage Stress Test — What Banks Check

Since 2018, all Canadian mortgage applicants must pass a stress test: they must qualify at the higher of: — Their actual interest rate + 2%, OR — 5.25% (the floor rate)

Example: You get offered a 5.5% mortgage rate. You must qualify at: 5.5% + 2% = 7.5%

This means your maximum mortgage at 7.5% qualification might be $580,000 even though your actual payments would be based on 5.5%.

Why this matters: If you make $120,000/year household income, the stress test limits your maximum purchase to roughly 4.5–5× your income, or about $540,000–$600,000 in most markets.

Tips to pass the stress test: — Pay down existing debts (car loans, credit cards) before applying — Increase down payment (lower mortgage = easier to qualify) — Get pre-approved 120 days before looking at homes

Closing Costs — The Budget Most Buyers Forget

First-time buyers often budget for the down payment and forget closing costs, which typically run 3–5% of purchase price.

Typical closing costs on a $700,000 Ontario home: — Land transfer tax (Ontario): ~$10,475 — Toronto LTT (if applicable): ~$10,275 — Legal fees: $1,500–$2,500 — Title insurance: $300–$500 — Home inspection: $400–$600 — Appraisal fee: $300–$500 — Moving costs: $1,000–$3,000 — Immediate repairs/furniture: $2,000–$10,000+ — CMHC insurance (if <20% down): Added to mortgage — HST on new construction: 13% on purchase price (significant!)

Budget rule: Always have closing costs + 1–2% reserve beyond your down payment saved before making an offer.

🔍 Insurance

How to Review Your Insurance Annually

Your insurance needs change every year. An annual review takes 30 minutes and can save thousands — while making sure you're actually protected when it matters.

Why Annual Insurance Reviews Matter

Most Canadians buy insurance once and forget it. This is a costly mistake. Life changes constantly — marriage, new child, home purchase, raise, retirement — and insurance needs change with it. An insurance policy that was perfect 3 years ago may be dangerously inadequate or unnecessarily expensive today.

The two review failures:
— Under-insured: Your income doubled, your house appreciated $200,000, you had two more children — but your life insurance hasn't changed in 8 years. If you die, your family gets a fraction of what they need.
— Over-insured: Kids are grown, mortgage is paid, you're wealthy — but you're still paying $300/month for life insurance you no longer need. That's $3,600/year wasted.

What to review annually:
— Life insurance: amount adequate? Term still appropriate?
— Home insurance: replacement cost updated for inflation and renovations?
— Auto insurance: right coverage level for vehicle age and value?
— Disability insurance: income coverage keeping pace with salary growth?
— Health/dental: employer coverage changed?
— Critical illness: still appropriate for your age and health concerns?

Best timing: Every January (align with tax season and year-end changes) or when you have a major life event. Set a calendar reminder — it takes less than 30 minutes if your info is organized.

Life Insurance Review — The Checklist

Step 1 — Recalculate your coverage need: A simple formula: 10x annual income + all debts + future education costs for children + immediate expenses ($25,000 buffer). If income is $80,000: $800,000 + mortgage $450,000 + RESP shortfall $80,000 + immediate costs $25,000 = $1,355,000 needed. Is your current coverage close?

Step 2 — Check beneficiary designations: Outdated beneficiaries are a major estate planning failure. Common mistakes: ex-spouse still named, parent named when you now have children, estate named (triggers probate fees and delays). Update beneficiaries every 3–5 years and after every major life event.

Step 3 — Review your term length: 20-year term at 30 means you're covered until 50. But your mortgage runs to 55 and your youngest won't finish university until you're 54. Consider adding another layer of coverage or extending the term before your current policy expires (renewing is very expensive — re-applying while healthy is far cheaper).

Step 4 — Check if employer group coverage changed: Companies reduce benefits during restructuring. Verify your current group life amount, AD&D, and disability coverage every year — don't assume it's the same as last year.

Step 5 — Consider convertibility: If your term policy is convertible (allows converting to permanent without new medical underwriting), know the deadline. If your health has deteriorated, converting before the deadline locks in permanent coverage you might not otherwise qualify for.

Home and Auto Review — What to Update

Home insurance review:
— Replacement cost vs market value: Insurance should cover what it costs to rebuild your home, not what you could sell it for. In high-cost cities, rebuild cost and market value are very different. Construction costs in Canada rose 20–30% since 2020 — your 2019 coverage amount is likely insufficient today.
— Renovations: Added a finished basement, new kitchen, or major addition? Call your insurer immediately. Unreported improvements may not be covered — or your entire claim could be disputed if you didn't disclose the changes.
— High-value items: Jewellery, electronics, musical instruments, artwork, and collectibles often have sub-limits ($2,000–$5,000) under standard policies. A separately scheduled "floater" covers them at full value.
— Sewer backup and overland water: Climate change is making flooding more common. Standard home insurance doesn't cover flood damage — you need separate sewer backup and/or overland water endorsements ($200–$400/year extra). Worth it in flood-prone areas.

Auto insurance review:
— Collision coverage on old vehicles: If your car is worth $5,000 and collision costs $600/year with a $1,000 deductible, you're insuring $4,000 of value for $600/year. When the car is worth under $5,000–$7,000, dropping collision often makes financial sense.
— New car: Immediately update your policy when you buy a vehicle — don't wait. Driving an uninsured new vehicle is illegal and uncovered.

Your 30-Minute Annual Insurance Audit

What to gather before starting:
— All current insurance policy documents
— Most recent pay stub (for income verification)
— Current mortgage statement
— List of major assets (home value, investments)
— Names and relationships of intended beneficiaries

The review (set 30 minutes, do it in one sitting):
1. Life: Check total coverage vs calculated need. Check beneficiaries. Note policy expiry dates.
2. Disability: Check % of income covered (should be 60–70% of gross). Check elimination period (90 days is typical).
3. Home: Confirm replacement cost amount. Confirm endorsements (sewer backup, water). Note any unreported renovations.
4. Auto: Check annual km, confirm no unreported drivers, review collision for older vehicles.
5. Health/dental: Confirm employer group coverage. Identify gaps (orthodontics, physiotherapy, vision).
Total time: 20–30 minutes once you have documents ready.

When to call your broker vs when to shop: Call your existing broker to update existing policies (adding coverage, updating beneficiaries). Shop competing quotes every 2–3 years for auto and home — loyalty doesn't always get you the best rate, and the savings from switching can be $500–$1,500/year. Life insurance rates don't change once locked in, but if your health has improved significantly, re-applying can get better rates.

Your First 30 Days — The Non-Negotiable Steps

The first month in Canada sets the foundation for everything that follows.

Day 1-7 priorities: Get your SIN (Social Insurance Number) from Service Canada. Bring proof of status (PR card, work permit, etc). Free and takes 20 minutes. You CANNOT work, open accounts, or access benefits without this. Open a no-fee bank account. Royal Bank, Scotiabank, and TD all have New to Canada packages. Bring your SIN, passport, and proof of address. Get a Canadian SIM card (Koodo, Fido, or Freedom Mobile — more affordable than Rogers/Bell/Telus)

Week 2-4 priorities: Apply for a Newcomer credit card (Scotiabank Welcome, TD Secured, or Capital One Secured). Start building Canadian credit immediately. Register your address with CRA (Canada Revenue Agency) to start receiving benefit payments. Research your provincial healthcare card — there is typically a 3-month waiting period (in Ontario). Get visitor health insurance to cover this gap.

Understanding the Canadian Financial System

Canada's banking system is organized and well-regulated — but different from India, the Philippines, or most other countries.

The Big 5 Banks (safest, most branches): Royal Bank (RBC), Toronto-Dominion (TD), Scotiabank (BNS), Bank of Montreal (BMO), CIBC. All deposits insured by CDIC up to $100,000.

Better options for daily banking (no fees): EQ Bank: No fees, 3-4% interest on savings, great for emergency funds. Tangerine: No fees, simple app, good for everyday banking. Simplii Financial: No monthly fees, tied to CIBC network.

How Canadian credit works: Your Indian/Filipino/UK credit history does NOT transfer to Canada. You start at zero. Equifax and TransUnion track your Canadian credit from the moment you open accounts.

The three things that build credit fastest: 1. Secured credit card — deposit $500, get $500 limit. Use it for groceries, pay in full monthly. 2. Cell phone plan in your name (not prepaid) 3. Apartment lease in your name

Timeline: With a secured card and consistent payments, most newcomers reach 650+ credit in 12 months, 700+ in 24 months.

TFSA and RRSP — Open These Immediately

These two accounts should be opened in your first month, even if you have $0 to put in them.

TFSA (Tax-Free Savings Account): Available to any Canadian resident with a SIN, age 18+. Your contribution room accumulates from the date you become a Canadian resident. 2024 room: $7,000. Start accumulating the day you arrive — you can never get those years back. Even with $50/month, start investing in a 100% equity all-in-one ETF or a savings account inside the TFSA.

Why open it now: Every year the TFSA is NOT open, you lose that contribution room forever. A newcomer who waits 5 years to open their TFSA loses $35,000+ in contribution room.

RRSP (Retirement Savings): Opens contribution room after your first year filing taxes in Canada (18% of earned income). Less urgent to open immediately, but important to know about. Particularly valuable once you are earning $60,000+.

Where to open: Wealthsimple (free, simple app) or your bank. Opening takes 10 minutes online.

Benefits You Are Entitled to as a Newcomer

Many newcomers do not know about the government benefits they qualify for. Some are available immediately upon getting PR; others require filing a tax return.

File a tax return EVERY YEAR, even with $0 income. This unlocks:

Canada Child Benefit (CCB): If you have children under 18, this can be $500-$700+/month per child depending on income. Apply immediately after getting PR.

GST/HST Credit: Paid quarterly (up to $519/year individual, $680/year couple) to offset consumption taxes. Automatically assessed when you file your taxes.

Canada Workers Benefit: If you are working and earning less than $43,000, you may receive up to $1,428/year.

Newcomer to Canada Pro Tip: File your first tax return even if you arrived mid-year and worked for only a few months. The benefits are retroactive to the date you became a resident.

Dental Care and Pharmacare: As of 2024, the Canadian Dental Care Plan covers uninsured Canadians under specific income thresholds. Apply at canada.ca/dental.

Sending Money Home — Remittances the Right Way

Most newcomers send money to family in India or elsewhere regularly. The Big Banks charge 3-5% in fees and poor exchange rates. Here is how to do it for less.

Best platforms for India remittances (2024): Wise (formerly TransferWise): Mid-market rate plus 0.5-1% fee. Best for amounts under $5,000. Arrive in 1-2 business days. Remitly: Competitive rates, express (same-day) or economy (3 days) options. ICICI Bank Canada: Good for large amounts, especially if family has ICICI India.

Calculate the REAL cost: Do not just look at the fee — look at the exchange rate offered vs. the Google mid-market rate. A 0% fee with a 3% worse rate costs more than a 1% fee at mid-market.

Monthly transfer strategy: If sending regularly, send larger amounts less frequently. Most platforms charge per transfer, so one $2,000 transfer beats four $500 transfers.

Tax note: Sending money from your Canadian income to India is NOT taxable in Canada — you have already paid Canadian income tax on it. However, income earned in India while you are a Canadian resident may need to be reported.

The SMART Framework for Financial Goals

Vague goals fail. 'I want to save more money' is not a goal — it's a wish. Here is how to make financial goals that actually get achieved.

SMART Goals: Specific: Not 'save money' but 'save $15,000 for a car down payment' Measurable: 'Save $625/month for 24 months' — trackable Achievable: Realistic given your income and expenses Relevant: Aligned with your actual life priorities Time-bound: 'By December 2026' not 'someday'

The five categories of financial goals: 1. Emergency: 3-6 month emergency fund (defensive) 2. Short-term (1-3 years): Car, vacation, home down payment 3. Medium-term (3-7 years): Home purchase, business start 4. Long-term (7+ years): Children's education (RESP), retirement 5. Legacy: Estate, inheritance, charity

The mistake most Canadians make: Setting ONE big vague goal instead of layering small concrete targets that build toward it. Example: Goal is to own a home. Concrete: 'Open FHSA this month. Contribute $500/month. In 5 years, have $40,000 saved.'

The Savings Rate — The Single Number That Matters Most

Your savings rate — the percentage of income you keep — is the most powerful determinant of when you reach financial goals.

The math: Save 10% of income: retire in roughly 43 years Save 20% of income: retire in roughly 37 years Save 30% of income: retire in roughly 28 years Save 50% of income: retire in roughly 17 years

The relationship is exponential, not linear. Going from 10% to 20% does not just speed up your retirement by 10% — it cuts 6 years off.

How to calculate your savings rate: (Monthly savings + investments) divided by monthly take-home pay times 100

Example: Amrit takes home $5,200/month. He contributes $500 TFSA plus $200 RRSP plus $100 savings = $800 saved. Savings rate = $800 divided by $5,200 = 15.4%

Target for different life stages: In your 20s: Aim for 10-15% (paying off student debt is OK to prioritize). In your 30s: Aim for 15-25% (peak earning years, maximize registered accounts). In your 40s: Aim for 25-35% (kids in school, pre-retirement runway). In your 50s: Aim for 30-40%+ (final accumulation phase).

Goal Stacking — Doing Multiple Goals Simultaneously

You do not have to choose between paying off debt, building an emergency fund, saving for a home, and investing for retirement. You can do all of them at once — with the right system.

The Priority Waterfall: 1. Capture employer RRSP match first (it is a 50-100% instant return) 2. Emergency fund to $1,000 (minimum buffer before anything else) 3. Pay off any debt over 8% interest (guaranteed risk-free return) 4. Build emergency fund to 3 months 5. Max FHSA if first-time buyer ($8,000/year, tax deductible) 6. Max TFSA ($7,000/year) 7. Max RRSP (if income above $60K) 8. Invest in non-registered account

The allocation trick: Divide your monthly savings capacity between all active goals by priority. Example with $800/month available: $100 to emergency top-up $300 to high-interest debt $400 to FHSA/TFSA

As each bucket fills, redirect those funds down the waterfall.

Tracking Progress — What Gets Measured Gets Done

You cannot hit a target you are not aiming at. Setting goals without tracking is the most common reason people fail.

Net worth tracking (monthly): List all assets (cash, investments, home equity) minus all debts. Update every month. Over time, watching this number grow is the most motivating thing in personal finance.

Simple tools: Spreadsheet (Google Sheets): Most powerful, fully customizable. Wealthsimple app: Tracks TFSA, RRSP automatically. Mint or YNAB: Budget and net worth tracking.

The monthly financial review: 1. Did I hit my savings target? (yes/no) 2. Where did I overspend? (identify one category) 3. Is my net worth higher than last month? (yes/no) 4. One micro-improvement for next month

Annual financial review (December or January): Review progress on 1, 3, and 5-year goals. Check if TFSA and RRSP contributions are on track. Update beneficiaries on accounts. Review insurance coverage vs. current life situation. Adjust savings rates based on income changes.

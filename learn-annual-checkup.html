💰Income & Taxes

Once a year — ideally in January or February — sit down for 2–3 hours and review every area of your financial life. Most Canadians skip this and lose thousands of dollars annually through forgotten subscriptions, unused contribution room, outdated insurance, and misaligned investments.

Start with your income and tax situation. Did you change jobs, get a raise, or start freelancing? Each change affects your optimal RRSP vs TFSA strategy. Log into CRA My Account at canada.ca and check your RRSP contribution room. Many Canadians carry unused room worth tens of thousands of dollars — every year you wait to use it costs you in taxes.

If your income increased significantly, consider making an RRSP contribution before the March 1 deadline to reduce this year's taxable income. A $10,000 RRSP contribution at a 43% marginal tax rate returns $4,300 in tax refund — money that should immediately go back into your TFSA or investments.

Check if you're eligible for the FHSA (First Home Savings Account) if you don't own a home. You can contribute $8,000/year (lifetime max $40,000) and get both a tax deduction and tax-free growth. This is one of the best accounts available to first-time buyers — many Canadians aren't using it.

🛡️Insurance & Protection

Insurance is the foundation that protects everything you've built. Most families buy insurance once and never review it — which means they're often underinsured as their lives change.

Life insurance review: If you bought term life 5 years ago and your income has doubled, your family's needs have doubled too. A common rule: 10–15× your annual income in coverage. If you earn $80,000, you should have $800,000–$1.2M in coverage. Review whether your current policy matches this.

Beneficiary designations: This is the most neglected task in Canadian finance. If you got divorced, had children, or your parents passed away, your old beneficiaries may still be named. A beneficiary who predeceases you and isn't updated means your insurance payout goes through probate — delaying distribution to your family by months and costing legal fees.

Disability insurance: Your most valuable asset is your ability to earn income. If you're a non-union employee, check whether your employer's group disability covers 60–70% of your income. Many plans only cover base salary, not overtime or bonuses. Self-employed Canadians have no employer plan at all — private disability insurance is critical.

Will and Power of Attorney: If you had a child, bought property, or went through a major life change, your will needs updating. A will costs $500–$1,500 with a lawyer — a fraction of the estate disputes that happen without one.

💳Debt & Credit

Your annual checkup should include pulling your credit report. Both Borrowell and Credit Karma offer free Equifax and TransUnion reports in Canada. Check for errors — incorrect late payments, fraudulent accounts, or collections you didn't know about. Disputing an error can improve your score 30–50 points, affecting your mortgage rate significantly.

List every debt you carry with its interest rate. Then calculate: how much interest did you pay last year on consumer debt? If that number is above $1,000, you have an urgent optimization problem. At the 2024 prime rate, many Canadians are paying 7–10% on lines of credit and 19.99–22.99% on credit cards — these returns are guaranteed and immediate when you pay them down.

Check whether a balance transfer opportunity exists. Several Canadian cards offer 0% balance transfer promotions for 6–12 months. Moving $5,000 in credit card debt from 19.99% to 0% for 12 months saves you ~$1,000 in interest — a real, guaranteed return. Apply the savings directly to principal during that window.

📈Investments

Review your investment allocation against your stated risk tolerance and time horizon. After a strong bull market, your 60/40 stock/bond allocation may have drifted to 75/25 — more risk than you intended. Annual rebalancing corrects this by selling what's grown and buying what hasn't, enforcing a "buy low, sell high" discipline automatically.

Review fees paid in the last 12 months. Check every account statement for management fees, fund MERs (Management Expense Ratios), and trading commissions. A 2% annual fee on $100,000 costs $2,000/year. Switching to low-cost ETFs (0.1–0.25% MER) available through Questrade or Wealthsimple Trade could save you $1,750 annually — guaranteed, risk-free savings.

Verify your registered accounts are optimized: TFSA for highest-growth investments (growth compounds tax-free), RRSP for income-earning assets (bonds, REITs), and non-registered accounts for Canadian dividend-paying stocks (eligible dividend tax credit makes these most tax-efficient in taxable accounts).

🎯Goals & Net Worth

The annual checkup is the right time to reassess your financial goals. Did you accomplish what you set out to do last year? What changed? Did your priority shift from saving for a home to saving for your children's education? Life changes faster than most financial plans.

Set concrete, numbered goals for the next 12 months: "Save $15,000 in TFSA," "Pay off $8,000 on line of credit," "Get life insurance quote." Vague goals like "save more" consistently underperform specific ones. Write the numbers down and revisit them at the next annual checkup.

If you don't have a written net worth statement, create one now: list all assets (chequing, savings, investments, property value, car value) and all liabilities (mortgage, line of credit, credit cards, student loans, car loan). Subtract liabilities from assets. This single number — your net worth — is the scorecard of all financial decisions. Track it annually, ideally quarterly.

🤔What Is Inflation?

Inflation is the rate at which the general price level of goods and services rises over time, eroding the purchasing power of money. When inflation is 3%, something that costs $100 today will cost $103 in one year. Over 20 years at 3% inflation, that $100 item costs $181. Your $100 bill still says $100 — but it buys significantly less.

Statistics Canada measures inflation through the Consumer Price Index (CPI), which tracks the average price change of a "basket" of goods and services representing typical Canadian household spending: food, shelter, transportation, health care, recreation, and clothing. The CPI basket is updated every few years to reflect actual spending patterns.

Canada's official inflation target is 2% annually, set by the Bank of Canada. From 2022–2023, inflation surged to 8.1% (June 2022) — the highest rate in over 40 years — driven primarily by supply chain disruptions from COVID-19, commodity price shocks from the Russia-Ukraine war, and pent-up consumer demand. By late 2024, inflation had returned closer to the 2% target.

💸How Inflation Affects Your Finances

Savings accounts: If your savings account pays 1% interest and inflation is 3%, your purchasing power is declining by 2% annually. Your balance grows in dollar terms but shrinks in real terms. This is called a negative real rate of return. At 2024 HISA rates (4–5%), savers actually earned a positive real return — unusual and temporary.

Fixed income (bonds, GICs): Inflation is particularly damaging to fixed-rate investments. A 5-year GIC locked at 3% during a period of 6% inflation loses 3% of purchasing power annually. The nominal return looks fine; the real return is negative. This is why financial planners recommend holding inflation-sensitive assets (stocks, real estate) alongside fixed income.

Wages: If your salary doesn't increase at least as fast as inflation, you've received an effective pay cut. Annual cost-of-living adjustments (COLA) in employment contracts typically reference CPI. Negotiating salary increases in line with or above inflation is important for maintaining real purchasing power over a career.

Mortgages and debt: Inflation actually benefits borrowers with fixed-rate debt. If you have a $400,000 mortgage at 3% fixed and inflation runs at 5%, you're paying back cheaper dollars. The real burden of your debt declines. This is why long-term fixed-rate mortgages can be advantageous in inflationary environments.

🛡️Protecting Yourself Against Inflation

Invest in equities: Stocks represent ownership in businesses that can raise prices along with inflation, pass increased costs to consumers, and grow revenues in nominal terms. Over long periods, equity markets have consistently outperformed inflation. The S&P/TSX Composite has averaged ~7% annually over the past 30 years against average inflation of ~2.5% — a real return of ~4.5%.

Real estate: Property values and rental income historically track or exceed inflation over the long run. As construction costs rise with inflation, replacing existing housing becomes more expensive, supporting prices. However, real estate is illiquid, leveraged, and requires significant maintenance — it's not the same as a passive inflation hedge.

TIPS and Real Return Bonds: The Government of Canada issues Real Return Bonds (RRBs) whose principal is adjusted with CPI inflation. If inflation runs 4%, your bond's principal grows 4%. These are available through full-service brokers — appropriate for retirees seeking inflation protection in fixed income.

I-bonds equivalent in Canada: Unlike the US, Canada doesn't have retail inflation-indexed savings bonds. The closest equivalent for everyday Canadians is holding short-term GICs or HISA and rolling them regularly as rates adjust upward with inflation, rather than locking into multi-year fixed rates.

🏦The Bank of Canada's Role

The Bank of Canada uses the overnight interest rate — the rate at which major banks lend to each other overnight — as its primary tool to control inflation. Raising this rate makes borrowing more expensive throughout the economy, reducing consumer and business spending, which slows demand and eases price pressure. Lowering rates does the opposite.

The transmission mechanism from BoC rate changes to your daily life: Bank of Canada raises overnight rate → banks raise prime rate → variable mortgage rates, HELOCs, and lines of credit increase → consumers have less disposable income → spending decreases → inflation slows. This process takes 12–18 months to work fully through the economy.

For Canadian households, the most direct impact of BoC rate decisions is on variable-rate mortgages and home equity lines of credit. Between March 2022 and July 2023, the BoC raised rates 10 consecutive times — from 0.25% to 5.0%. Variable mortgage holders saw monthly payments increase by $800–$2,000+ on a $600,000 mortgage, dramatically reshaping household budgets.

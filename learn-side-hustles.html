Side Hustles in Canada: How to Earn More and Tax It Right

A side income can dramatically accelerate wealth-building — if you structure it properly and understand the tax rules.

💼 High-Potential Side Hustles for Canadians

Digital services (highest margin): Freelance writing, graphic design, web development, video editing, social media management, virtual assistance, online tutoring. Low startup costs, work from anywhere, scalable to significant income.

Ride-share and delivery: Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, Uber Eats, Instacart. Low barrier to entry — you need a car and a clean driving record. Income varies by city and hours; expect $18-$28/hour before expenses in major Canadian cities.

Skilled trades and services: Plumbing, electrical, carpentry, painting, landscaping, cleaning. High demand, strong hourly rates ($60-$150+/hour for skilled work), and relatively low competition for quality providers.

Rental income: Renting a basement suite, room, or parking spot. Lower time commitment than active side hustles; generates income while you sleep.

Digital products and content: Online courses, ebooks, YouTube monetization, podcast sponsorships. High upfront effort, potentially passive income long-term.

📊 Tax Obligations — No Threshold Below Which You're Exempt

All side hustle income is taxable in Canada and must be reported on your T1 tax return — there is no minimum threshold. A teenager making $500 selling handmade bracelets on Etsy technically has taxable income. CRA has increasingly sophisticated digital income tracking and cross-references with platform data.

Self-employment income is reported on Schedule T2125. It's added to your other income (employment income, etc.) and taxed at your marginal rate. However, you can deduct all legitimate business expenses against this income, potentially reducing your taxable self-employment income substantially.

💰 Deductible Business Expenses

Vehicle (for delivery/ride-share/on-site services): Deduct the business-use percentage of gas, insurance, maintenance, registration, and CCA on the vehicle. Requires a mileage log documenting business vs. personal trips — CRA regularly audits vehicle deductions without logs.

Home office (for digital work): Deduct the proportional share of rent/mortgage interest, utilities, and internet for a dedicated workspace — typically calculated as (office square footage ÷ total home square footage) × annual costs.

Equipment and software: Computer, camera, microphone, software subscriptions, external drives — all deductible in the year purchased (if under $500) or as CCA over multiple years.

Phone plan: The business-use percentage of your phone bill is deductible. If you use your phone 60% for business, deduct 60% of the annual cost.

🧾 GST/HST: The $30,000 Threshold

Once your side hustle earns over $30,000 in any 12-month rolling period, you must register for GST/HST with CRA, begin charging 13% HST to Ontario clients (or the applicable provincial rate), and file quarterly or annual HST returns.

Before hitting $30,000: you're a 'small supplier' and are not required to charge HST. Voluntarily registering early allows you to claim input tax credits (recover the HST you pay on business expenses) — often worth doing once your business expenses are significant.

🏢 Sole Proprietor vs. Corporation

Most side hustlers operate as sole proprietors — no registration required, income reported on your personal return, simple. Incorporation becomes worth considering when your side hustle earns $70,000-$100,000+ annually, because the small business corporate tax rate in Ontario (~12.2%) is dramatically lower than personal marginal rates of 43-53%. Consult a CPA before incorporating — the setup and ongoing accounting costs need to be weighed against the tax savings for your specific income level.

🏦The Big Banks

Canada's banking system is dominated by five major banks: RBC (Royal Bank of Canada), TD Bank, Scotiabank, BMO (Bank of Montreal), and CIBC (Canadian Imperial Bank of Commerce). Together they hold over 80% of Canadian banking assets. A sixth major institution, National Bank, dominates in Quebec. These banks are highly regulated by the Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions (OSFI) and deposits are insured by CDIC (Canada Deposit Insurance Corporation) up to $100,000 per category per institution.

The Big Five offer comprehensive services: chequing and savings accounts, credit cards, personal and home equity loans, mortgages, investment accounts, and insurance. Their primary advantages are physical branch networks (valuable for complex transactions), integrated digital platforms connecting all your accounts, and broad ATM networks. Their disadvantages are higher monthly fees, lower savings rates, and higher investment fees compared to alternatives.

For newcomers to Canada, every Big Five bank offers a newcomer banking package with reduced or waived fees for the first year, no minimum balance requirements, and sometimes a welcome bonus. These programs make it easy to establish Canadian banking history from day one, which matters for credit building and eventually mortgage applications.

💡Alternatives to Big Banks

Digital-first banks — EQ Bank, Simplii Financial, and Tangerine — offer no-fee or low-fee banking with competitive interest rates on savings. These are legitimate, regulated institutions (Simplii is backed by CIBC; Tangerine by Scotiabank) with CDIC deposit protection. Their limitation is no physical branches, which matters for specific transactions but rarely affects daily banking.

Credit unions operate in every province and often offer better rates and lower fees than major banks. Meridian Credit Union in Ontario, FirstOntario, and Desjardins in Quebec are among the largest. Member-owned structure means profits return to members rather than shareholders. Deposits are covered by provincial deposit insurance rather than CDIC — equivalent protection.

For investing accounts specifically, discount brokerages (Questrade, Wealthsimple Trade, National Bank Direct Brokerage) offer commission-free or low-cost trading unavailable at bank branches. Many Canadians use a combination: big bank for mortgage and chequing, digital bank for high-interest savings, and discount brokerage for investments.

📋Types of Accounts

Chequing account: Your primary day-to-day spending account. Accepts direct deposit, processes bill payments, supports Interac e-Transfers and debit purchases. Usually pays little or no interest. May charge monthly fees if balance thresholds aren't met.

Savings account (HISA): Higher Interest Savings Account. Earns interest on your balance, typically 3–5% at digital banks (2024 rates) versus 0.01–1% at big bank standard savings. Intended for money you're not spending immediately — emergency funds, short-term savings goals. Transaction limits may apply (some have Reg E-style restrictions).

Registered accounts: TFSA (Tax-Free Savings Account), RRSP (Registered Retirement Savings Plan), FHSA (First Home Savings Account), RESP (Registered Education Savings Plan). These are not "bank accounts" per se — they're government-registered account types that can hold cash, GICs, mutual funds, or (at brokerages) stocks and ETFs. The registration provides specific tax benefits.

GIC (Guaranteed Investment Certificate): A fixed-term deposit paying guaranteed interest. Your money is locked in for a specified term (30 days to 5 years) in exchange for a guaranteed return. CDIC-insured. Best rates available online at comparison sites like RateHub or Cannex.

💸Common Fees to Watch

The most important fees to understand: monthly account fee ($0–$17/month, waivable with minimum balance), NSF fee ($45–$48 per bounced payment), e-Transfer fee ($0–$1.50 depending on account tier), ATM fees at non-network machines ($2–$5 per withdrawal plus operator surcharge), and wire transfer fees ($15–$50 for international transfers).

Always review your bank statement monthly and question any fee you don't recognize. Many Canadians pay hundreds of dollars annually in fees they could eliminate with a 15-minute call to their bank to either get the fee waived or switch to an appropriate account tier. Call the bank's retention line and ask specifically: "Is there a way to reduce or eliminate these fees?"

🛡️Your Money Is Protected

Canadian bank deposits are protected by CDIC (Canada Deposit Insurance Corporation), a federal Crown corporation. CDIC covers up to $100,000 per depositor per category at each member institution. Categories include: deposits in one name, deposits in joint names, RRSP deposits, TFSA deposits, RRIF deposits, and RESP deposits. This means a single person can have up to $700,000+ protected at one institution by holding funds across multiple categories.

CDIC covers cash deposits, GICs with terms of 5 years or less, and savings accounts. It does not cover stocks, ETFs, mutual funds, foreign currency deposits, or GICs with terms over 5 years. Credit union deposits are covered by provincial deposit insurance schemes — equivalent protection but administered provincially rather than federally. No Canadian bank covered by CDIC has failed since CDIC's creation in 1967.

OAS for Immigrants and Newcomers in Canada

Old Age Security rules for immigrants are more complex than for lifelong Canadians. Partial pensions, international agreements, and residency rules all affect what you'll receive.

How OAS Works for Immigrants — The Residency Rules

Old Age Security (OAS) is Canada's universal pension paid to residents 65+. Unlike CPP (based on contributions), OAS is based on years of Canadian residency. This makes the rules very different for immigrants than for lifelong Canadians.

Full OAS requires 40 years of Canadian residency after age 18. If you lived in Canada for all 40 years from age 18 to 65, you receive the full OAS benefit ($713/month in mid-2024).

Partial OAS for those with fewer years: You need a minimum of 10 years of Canadian residency after age 18 to qualify for any OAS while living in Canada. Your benefit is prorated:
— 10 years = 10/40 = 25% of full OAS = ~$178/month
— 20 years = 20/40 = 50% = ~$357/month
— 30 years = 30/40 = 75% = ~$535/month
— 40+ years = 100% = ~$713/month

Living outside Canada: To receive OAS while living outside Canada permanently, you need 20 years of Canadian residency after age 18. This is important for immigrants who plan to return to their home country in retirement.

Social Security Agreements — The International Factor

Canada has social security agreements with over 50 countries that help immigrants qualify for pensions they otherwise wouldn't. These agreements allow "totalization" — combining years of residency/contributions in both countries to meet minimum thresholds.

How it works: Harjot immigrated from India at age 45 and retired at 65 — only 20 years of Canadian residency. Normally he'd get 50% OAS. But if India had a social security agreement with Canada, his working years in India could count toward qualifying for OAS (not toward the benefit amount — just the minimum threshold). Note: Canada does not currently have a social security agreement with India — this is significant for many Punjabi immigrants.

Countries Canada HAS agreements with (relevant examples): United States, United Kingdom, Australia, Germany, Netherlands, Italy, Greece, Portugal, Spain, and many others. Check canada.ca for the full list — it changes as new agreements are signed.

If your home country has an agreement: Contact Service Canada to understand how your foreign pension/contribution years count. You may qualify for partial OAS sooner, and your home country may also pay you a partial pension based on Canadian years.

For Indian immigrants specifically: Without a Canada-India social security agreement, your years in India don't count toward OAS. Only Canadian residency years matter. This makes arriving earlier in life much more financially important for OAS purposes.

Maximizing OAS Despite Limited Canadian Residency

Delay OAS to 70: Every month you delay OAS past 65, the benefit increases by 0.6% (7.2%/year). Delaying to 70 gives you 36% more than at 65. For someone with partial OAS (say 50% of full = $357/month at 65), delaying to 70 increases it to $485/month — $128 more per month for life.
Breakeven age for this delay: approximately 82–83.

Maximize Canadian residency years before retirement: If you're considering semi-retirement or extended travel outside Canada, be aware that years spent mostly outside Canada don't count as Canadian residency years for OAS purposes. Stay in Canada through your working years to maximize residency count.

Spouse with more Canadian residency years: A spouse who arrived younger or earlier will have more OAS. In a household where one spouse has full OAS and one has partial, pension income splitting and GIS optimization become important tax planning tools. Run the numbers with a retirement income specialist.

Other pensions from your home country: You may be entitled to a pension from India (EPFO pension for those who contributed), Philippines (SSS pension), UK (National Insurance pension if you worked there), or other countries. These are usually small but worth claiming. Many immigrants don't realize they qualify. Contact the relevant foreign government's pension authority directly.

GIS Eligibility and the OAS-GIS Combination for Immigrants

Many immigrants with partial OAS also qualify for the Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS) — especially if their partial OAS means total income is low. GIS is non-taxable and can significantly supplement a small OAS cheque.

Example: Navdeep arrived in Canada at 45. At 65, he has 20 years Canadian residency → 50% OAS = $357/month. He has minimal CPP (only 20 years of contributions). He has no workplace pension. His income is approximately $357/month + small CPP of ~$500/month = $857/month total. This is well below GIS thresholds — he qualifies for significant GIS supplement, potentially $700+/month additional. Combined: ~$1,550/month.

The TFSA strategy for GIS recipients: TFSA withdrawals don't count as income for GIS purposes. If Navdeep has $100,000 in TFSA, withdrawing $10,000/year has zero GIS impact. Withdrawing from RRSP instead would reduce his GIS dollar-for-dollar. Every immigrant with limited Canadian work history should prioritize TFSA over RRSP to preserve GIS eligibility.

Application process: Apply for OAS at Service Canada, 6–12 months before turning 65. GIS is assessed automatically once OAS is set up and you file your annual tax return. Must file taxes every year — even if income is below the filing threshold — to maintain GIS eligibility. Newcomer seniors who don't file taxes lose GIS. Help family members file even if they have no income.

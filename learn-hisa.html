High-Interest Savings Accounts (HISA) in Canada: Where to Park Your Short-Term Money

HISAs earn you real interest on cash you'll need within 5 years. Here's which ones are worth using and how to maximize them.

🏦 What Is a HISA?

A High-Interest Savings Account (HISA) is a deposit account at a regulated Canadian financial institution that pays a meaningfully higher interest rate than standard chequing or savings accounts at major banks. The typical savings rate at TD, RBC, or Scotiabank is 0.01-0.05% — essentially nothing. A competitive HISA pays 3-4.5% or more annually on the same CDIC-insured deposits.

HISAs are appropriate for: emergency funds (3-6 months of expenses), money you'll need in 1-3 years (house down payment, car purchase, education), and any cash that shouldn't be invested in the market because of its near-term use case. Don't use HISAs for money you won't need for 5+ years — that belongs in an equity investment account.

🏦 Best Canadian HISAs in 2024

EQ Bank Savings Plus Account: Consistently among the highest rates in Canada (currently in the 3.75-4.5% range). No monthly fees, no minimum balance, no transaction limits. CDIC insured. Easy ETF transfers to/from other institutions. For most Canadians, this is the primary HISA recommendation.

Tangerine Savings Account: Scotiabank subsidiary. Rates vary (often with promotional rates for new deposits of 4.5%+ for 5 months). Free transfers. Competitive ongoing rates though often not quite matching EQ Bank. Good if you prefer a larger institution.

Simplii Financial High-Interest Savings Account: CIBC's digital brand. Competitive rates (check current promotions). Easy integration if you bank at CIBC. Seamless transfers.

Oaken Financial: Consistently offers among the highest HISA rates. CDIC insured. Less well-known but a legitimate option for rate maximizers.

Wealthsimple Cash Account: Competitive rates. Integrated with Wealthsimple investing account — easy to move money between HISA and investment accounts. Good option for Wealthsimple users.

💡 TFSA HISA: Interest Without Tax

You can hold a HISA inside your TFSA — combining the TFSA's tax-free interest with the HISA's higher rate. Interest earned on a non-registered HISA is fully taxable in the year earned; inside a TFSA, the same interest is completely tax-free.

Example: $50,000 in a non-registered HISA at 4% = $2,000 interest, taxed at 43% marginal rate = $860 tax. After-tax: $1,140.
Same $50,000 in a TFSA HISA at 4% = $2,000 interest, zero tax. After-tax: $2,000.

Always fill your TFSA before opening a non-registered HISA for any money you're saving for medium-term goals. EQ Bank, Tangerine, and Oaken Financial all offer TFSA HISA accounts.

📊 HISA vs. GIC: The Trade-Off

GICs (Guaranteed Investment Certificates) offer locked-in rates for a fixed term — typically 6 months to 5 years. Current 1-year GIC rates are often slightly higher than HISA rates (5-5.5%+ for 1-year GICs at competitive institutions). The trade-off: your money is locked and you cannot access it without penalty during the term.

Use a HISA for your emergency fund and for money you might need on short notice. Use GICs for money you won't need for the full term — locking in a higher rate in exchange for the certainty you won't need early access. A GIC ladder (investing equal amounts in 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5-year GICs) provides both competitive rates and periodic access to portions of your savings as each GIC matures.

⚠️ What to Watch Out For

Promotional rates: Many banks offer elevated 'promotional' rates for 90-180 days to attract new deposits, then revert to much lower ongoing rates. Read the fine print and set a calendar reminder to check the rate at the promotional period's end.

Non-CDIC institutions: Some online-only HISAs are offered by non-CDIC members — check that your deposits are covered by CDIC or a provincial credit union deposit protection scheme before depositing significant amounts.

Holding too much cash: For money with a 5+ year time horizon, a HISA's 3-4% return is significantly below the historical 7-10% annual return of a diversified equity portfolio. Cash drag — holding excess money in savings when it should be invested — is a real wealth cost over long periods.
